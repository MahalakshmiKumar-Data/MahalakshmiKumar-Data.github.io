Data Visualisation · Power BI

Retail Sales Performance Analysis

Power BIDAXPower QueryExcelSQL

Project Overview

Explored transaction-level retail sales data in Power BI Desktop to generate insights for senior management. The analysis focuses on understanding overall sales and profitability, identifying time-based trends, comparing performance across regions and product categories, and assessing how discount levels influence profit.

Dataset

Transaction-level retail dataset covering 2021–2023 with fields including OrderID, OrderDate, ShipMode, CustomerSegment, geographic data (Country, State, City, Region), product details (Category, SubCategory), Quantity, UnitPrice, Discount, and Unit_Cost.

Data Preparation

  • Resolved OrderDate import issue — converted text column to Date using English (UK) locale in Power Query to ensure correct time-based analysis
  • Identified high-discount transactions (20–30%) as outliers; retained all rows and flagged their effect in discount-impact analysis

DAX Measures Created

  • Total Revenue = Quantity × UnitPrice × (1 − Discount)
  • Total Cost = Quantity × Unit_Cost
  • Total Profit = Total Revenue − Total Cost
  • Profit Margin % = Total Profit ÷ Total Revenue (returns BLANK on zero revenue to avoid misleading values)
  • Total Quantity Sold = SUM of Quantity
  • Number of Orders = DISTINCTCOUNT of OrderID

Key Insights

  • 0% and 1–10% discount bands deliver the highest profit and strongest margins — the safest pricing zones
  • Profit margin drops sharply in the 21–30% discount band, indicating aggressive discounting erodes profitability
  • Subcategories Chairs, Machines, and Furnishings show higher sensitivity to discounting and require pricing controls

Python Analysis

Insurance Charges Analysis

PythonPandasSeabornMatplotlibNumPy

Project Overview

Analysed 2,000+ insurance records to identify which demographic and health factors most strongly influence insurance charges. The goal was to provide actionable pricing insights for a health insurance company's underwriting and customer advisory teams.

Dataset

2,000+ records with fields: Age, Sex, BMI, Children, Smoker status, Region, and Charges. Dataset sourced from a real insurance dataset commonly used in actuarial analysis.

Methodology

  • Imported and inspected data using Pandas — checked dtypes, missing values, and descriptive statistics
  • Cleaned dataset: removed rows with zero or negative charges and invalid age entries
  • Engineered new features: BMI Category (Underweight / Normal / Overweight / Obese) and Age Group (Young / Middle-aged / Senior)
  • Created Matplotlib visualisations: bar chart (avg charges by region), line chart (avg charges by age), pie chart (smoker vs non-smoker)
  • Created Seaborn visualisations: histogram (charge distribution), boxplot (smoker vs non-smoker charges), scatter plot (BMI vs charges), correlation heatmap

Key Findings

  • Smoking status is the single strongest predictor of insurance charges — smokers pay significantly more across all age groups and BMI categories
  • Charges increase consistently with age, particularly for smokers in the Senior (60+) age group
  • Obese individuals who smoke represent the highest-risk and highest-cost segment
  • Regional variation exists but is secondary to smoking status and BMI as cost drivers

Sales Analytics · Excel

Bike Sales Dashboard

ExcelPivot TablesVLOOKUPINDEX MATCHCOUNTIFS

Project Overview

Built a dynamic Excel dashboard analysing 1,000+ customer records to understand what drives bike purchases across regions, income levels, and demographic segments. The analysis was designed to inform regional sales targeting.

Technical Approach

  • Used VLOOKUP and INDEX MATCH to cross-reference customer and sales data across multiple sheets
  • Built COUNTIFS formulas to segment purchase behaviour by region, gender, and income band
  • Created Pivot Tables to summarise purchases by region, age group, and commute distance
  • Designed an interactive dashboard with slicers for dynamic filtering

Key Findings

  • Europe (123 purchases) significantly outperformed Pacific (76 purchases) — suggesting stronger demand or better market fit in European markets
  • Middle-aged customers in mid-income brackets represented the highest-converting demographic segment
  • Short commute distances (0–1 miles) correlated with higher bike purchase rates — purchase was lifestyle, not purely commute-driven

SQL Analysis

Restaurant & Employee SQL Analysis

SQLCTEsWindow FunctionsMulti-table JOINsSubqueries

Project Overview

Wrote 15+ business SQL queries across two datasets — a restaurant menu/orders dataset and an employee/department dataset — to answer real-world pricing, customer, and workforce questions using advanced SQL techniques.

Restaurant Dataset — Business Questions Answered

  • How many items are on the menu, and what are the min/max prices?
  • How many luxury items exist, and how many items per category?
  • What is the average price per category?
  • Which items are priced below the average menu price? (Subquery)
  • Which customers have orders greater than $40, and what did they order? (JOIN + filter)
  • Who placed the highest-value order? (CTE approach)
  • Orders placed in Q1 (Jan–Mar) with delivery status classification (CTE + CASE)

Employee Dataset — Business Questions Answered

  • List employees with their department names (INNER JOIN — valid departments only)
  • All employees including those without a department (LEFT JOIN)
  • All departments including those with no employees (RIGHT JOIN)
  • Employees not assigned to any department (LEFT JOIN + NULL filter)
  • Departments with no employees assigned (RIGHT JOIN + NULL filter)
  • Department name with employee headcount (GROUP BY + COUNT)
  • Employees in the IT department (subquery filter)
  • Employees with no department or non-existent department (UNION / FULL OUTER JOIN)
